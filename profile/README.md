# Should I Stay In My Marriage or Leave (How Do You Know When to Leave a Marriage?)

Sitting across from someone who's asking this question is one of the hardest parts of my work. There's no worksheet that spits out an answer, no magic number of arguments that tips the scale. I often hear from people who've been turning this question over in their heads for months, sometimes years, afraid to say it out loud because saying it makes it real. If you're here reading this, you already know the weight of that question. You don't need me to tell you marriage is hard. You need something clearer than that. So let's walk through this together, honestly and without the fluff.

**You Feel Alone More Than You Feel Connected**

Marriage isn't about constant happiness. Anyone who tells you otherwise hasn't been married long. But there's a difference between the normal ebb of closeness and distance, and a pattern where you feel like a roommate instead of a partner. Ask yourself this: when something good happens in your day, is your spouse the first person you want to tell? When something bad happens, do you reach for them, or do you brace yourself for their reaction instead?

Loneliness inside a marriage is its own particular kind of pain. It's worse, in some ways, than being single, because you're surrounded by evidence of a partnership that isn't actually partnering. If you've stopped sharing your inner life with your spouse, if you've started keeping score of what you get versus what you give, that's worth sitting with. One rough patch doesn't mean the marriage is over. But months, or years, of feeling unseen? That's data. Don't ignore it just because it's uncomfortable.

**Your Values Have Split In Different Directions**

People change. That's not controversial. The question is whether you and your spouse are changing in ways that still fit together, or whether you're becoming two different people with two different sets of priorities. Maybe one of you wants children and the other doesn't anymore. Maybe your ideas about money, faith, honesty, or how to treat each other have drifted so far apart that compromise feels impossible rather than just difficult.

Here's a way to think about it. Small disagreements are about preferences. Big ones are about values. You can compromise on where to spend a holiday. It's much harder to compromise on whether lying is acceptable, or whether you want to build a life around the same core things. If you find yourselves arguing about the same fundamental issue over and over, with no movement, that repetition is telling you something. It's not that you fight. It's that the fights never resolve, because you're standing on different ground.

**Safety Is Not Up For Debate**

I want to be direct here, because this point matters more than any other on this list. If you are experiencing physical violence, threats, or a pattern of control that makes you afraid of your own spouse, this isn't a "should I stay or should I go" situation in the way the rest of this article is describing. Fear has no business being a permanent resident in your home. If you feel unsafe, trust that feeling. Your body knows things your mind hasn't fully caught up to yet.

This also applies to less visible forms of harm. Constant belittling, being cut off from your own money, having your decisions controlled down to the smallest detail. These patterns wear a person down slowly, and sometimes so slowly you don't notice how much of yourself you've lost. If any of this sounds familiar, please take it seriously. Your wellbeing isn't something to negotiate away.

**You've Tried To Fix It, And Nothing Has Changed**

This is the point where people often feel guilty, because they think leaving means they didn't try hard enough. But trying isn't just about time passing. It's about real effort, on both sides, aimed at real change. Have you had honest conversations about what's wrong? Have you both made changes, even small ones, based on those conversations? Or does every conversation end the same way, with promises that don't stick and behavior that snaps right back?

There's a particular kind of exhaustion that comes from trying alone. If you're the only one showing up to fix the marriage, if your spouse nods along and then does nothing differently, you're not actually working on the relationship together. You're carrying it by yourself. A marriage takes two people pulling in the same direction. If you've given real effort, over a real stretch of time, and things haven't budged, that's important information. It doesn't mean you failed. It means the relationship needs something you can't provide alone.

**You Can't Picture Five Years From Now, And That Scares You**

Try this exercise. Picture your life five years from now if nothing in your marriage changes. Not a better version, not a hopeful version. Just more of the same, exactly as it is today. How does that make you feel? Relieved? Neutral? Or does something in your chest tighten at the thought?

This isn't a scientific test, but it's a useful one. People often know the answer to the stay-or-leave question long before they're ready to admit it out loud. If picturing more years of the same situation fills you with dread, pay attention to that. If it fills you with a quiet sense of okay, that matters too. Don't dismiss your gut reaction just because your mind hasn't caught up with a tidy explanation yet.

**Your Sense Of Self Has Started To Disappear**

This one sneaks up on people. You don't wake up one day and decide to lose yourself in a marriage. It happens slowly, one small compromise at a time, until you look up one day and don't recognize who you've become. Ask yourself honestly: do you still feel like you? Do you still have opinions you're allowed to voice, interests you're allowed to pursue, a personality that isn't constantly filtered through what will keep the peace?

A good marriage should make you feel more like yourself, not less. It should give you room to grow, not shrink you down to fit inside someone else's comfort. If you've noticed yourself apologizing for things that don't need apologies, staying quiet about things that matter to you, or feeling like you're performing a version of yourself rather than living as yourself, that's worth real attention. Marriage is meant to be a partnership between two whole people, not one person slowly disappearing to keep another person comfortable.

Saving a marriage isn’t about pretending the pain never happened. It’s about deciding that your relationship is worth fighting for, even when things feel distant, broken, or uncertain.

Right now, you may feel exhausted from the arguments, the silence, or the growing gap between you and your spouse. Your marriage story does not have to end here.

No matter how disconnected things may feel today, healing is possible when both love and effort still exist beneath the hurt. Small changes can open the door to deeper conversations, renewed intimacy, and the kind of connection you thought was lost forever.
